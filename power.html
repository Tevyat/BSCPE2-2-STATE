THE INHERENT POWER

OF THE STATE

1. POLICE POWER




The police power in the Philippines refers to the plenary power vested in the legislature to make laws, statutes, and ordinances that promote the health, morals, peace, education, good order, safety, and general welfare of the people. This power is exercised by various entities, including the Philippine Congress, the President, executive agencies, and local government units like the Sangguniang Panlungsod of Manila.

The President can directly perform functions conferred upon agencies like the Department of Transportation and Communications (DOTC) under the police power, as it falls within the President's power of control over executive departments. Delegated powers to agencies like the DOTC to establish and administer programs are considered part of the nature of police power, aimed at promoting the welfare of the people.

WHO MAY EXERCISE THE POLICE POWER OF THE STATE?


The Philippine Congress

They have the authority to enact laws and regulations for the protection of public health, safety, morals, and general welfare.

The President and Executive Agencies

The President can exercise police power through executive orders and by directing the performance of duties by executive departments. Administrative agencies can also be delegated police power by the legislature.

Local Government Units (LGUs)

The Local Government Code delegates police power to LGUs, allowing their legislative bodies (Sanggunians) to enact ordinances for the general welfare of their constituents.

The President's authority to order the implementation of projects and the Secretary's power to establish and administer comprehensive and integrated rules and regulations have been delegated for the good and welfare of the people, partaking of the nature of police power.

While police power rests primarily with the legislature, it may be delegated, and by virtue of a valid delegation, the power may be exercised by the President, administrative boards, and the lawmaking bodies of municipalities.

LIMITATIONS OF THE
POLICE POWER


The police power of the state in the Philippines is subject to several limitations:

  • Constitutional limitations: The exercise of police power must not violate the rights guaranteed by the Philippine Constitution, such as due process, equal protection, and freedom of speech.
  • Reasonable relationship to a legitimate public purpose: The exercise of police power must be reasonably necessary and appropriate for achieving a legitimate public purpose, such as protecting public health, safety, or welfare.
  • >Non-arbitrary and non-oppressive: The measures taken under the police power must not be unfair, oppressive, partial, or discriminatory.
  • Consistency with public policy: Police power regulations must be general and consistent with public policy.
  • Reasonableness: The exercise of police power must be reasonable and not prohibit, but may regulate, trade.
  • Conformity with procedure: Police power regulations must be passed according to the procedure prescribed by law.



The Philippine Supreme Court plays a crucial role in interpreting the scope and limitations of the police power, ensuring that it is exercised within constitutional boundaries while balancing the state's interest in protecting public welfare with the rights of individuals and businesses.

The exercise of police power must not contravene the Constitution or any statute, be unfair or oppressive, be partial or discriminatory, prohibit but may regulate trade, be general and consistent with public policy, and must not be unreasonable. The City of Manila, for instance, has the power to enact ordinances under its police power, ensuring the general welfare of its inhabitants in line with the principle that the welfare of the people is the supreme law.

Overall, the police power in the Philippines is a broad authority aimed at promoting public welfare and maintaining order, subject to constitutional limitations and the requirement that its exercise be reasonable, fair, and in the best interest of the community.

2. POWER OF EMINENT DOMAIN

The power of eminent domain also called “condemnation” is the power of local, state, or federal government agencies to take private property for public use so long as the government pays just compensation. The government can exercies its power of eminent domain even if the owner does not wish to sell his or her property.

If the landowner refuses:

Governments have recourse when landowners refuse to sell their property. The government can initiate condemnation proceedings against the owner and property to secure the land.

3. POWER OF TAXATION




The power of taxation is a fundamental authority granted to governments to impose taxes on individuals, businesses, and other entities within their jurisdiction (Mikesell, 2014). It is considered one of the essential powers of the state and is crucial for funding public expenditures and providing essential services to citizens. Taxation serves various purposes, including revenue generation, redistribution of wealth, and regulation of economic activities.

LIFEBLOOD THEORY AND THE
DOCTRINE OF NECESSITY



The Lifeblood Theory of taxation asserts that taxation is the lifeblood of government, essential for its existence and functioning. This theory underscores the crucial role of taxation in funding governmental activities and ensuring the provision of public goods and services.

Meanwhile, the Doctrine of Necessity maintains that the power to tax is an inherent attribute of sovereignty, crucial for the state's survival and effective governance. This doctrine emphasizes the necessity of taxation to meet the financial needs of the government when necessary for the welfare and survival of the state, particularly during crises or emergencies.

It recognizes that there are situations where the government must act in the public interest, even if it means exceeding its ordinary powers or bypassing legal procedures. In the context of taxation, the Doctrine of Necessity may justify the imposition of emergency taxes or other extraordinary measures during times of crisis or national emergency.

BASIC PRINCIPLE OF
A SOUND TAX SYSTEM




The basic principle of a sound tax system is to achieve fairness, efficiency, simplicity, and adequacy (Bird & Zolt, 2019). A fair tax system ensures that taxes are imposed equitably, taking into account taxpayers' ability to pay and distributing the tax burden fairly across different income levels. An efficient tax system minimizes administrative costs and economic distortions while maximizing revenue collection. Simplicity in tax laws and procedures reduces compliance costs and improves taxpayer compliance. Adequacy ensures that tax revenues are sufficient to meet the government's expenditure requirements without resorting to excessive borrowing or deficit financing.
